# Voice profile — Priya Raman

## The one test that overrides everything
If it sounds like it could've come from any other applicant, it's wrong. I'd rather say one specific true thing
than three impressive-sounding ones.

## The core of it
Plain and direct — close to how I'd actually explain the work to someone I respect. Not formal-for-the-sake-of-it,
not casual either. My best samples are the emails where I'm explaining a decision I made.

## Cadence
Sentences that run a normal length and connect with commas and "and" — I don't write in short punchy bursts, and
choppy prose reads as fake to me. A short sentence is fine to land a point, but only next to a longer one.

## How it builds
Lead with the point, then back it up. In a cover letter: a real observation about the firm, then the one story
that proves I can do the work, then a direct ask. Emphasis comes from a concrete number or a contrast, never from
piling up adjectives.

## Texture
Concrete over abstract — "I built the bottoms-up demand model the recommendation rested on," not "I leveraged
analytical frameworks." Named things, real outcomes. A little dry honesty shows through ("I'll be upfront").

## Hedging
When I'm not sure, I say so plainly — "my read is," "I'd guess." I don't dress up a guess as a fact, and I don't
do the stiff corporate "it could be argued that."

## Openers
A specific, earned observation about the firm or the problem. Never "I am writing to express my interest in."

## Closers
A confident, short, warm ask. "Happy to chat whenever works." No begging, no "I'd be a great fit."

## The POV underneath
I believe the analysts worth hiring are the ones who own a piece of work all the way through and can defend the
number, not the ones who can recite frameworks. That belief shapes how I write about myself.

## Reference anchor
The way a good builder writes a project post-mortem — first-person, owns the decisions, plain about what worked
and what didn't. If a paragraph sounds like a press release, it's off-register.
